# Cold Outreach Pipeline for B2B SaaS Playbook

---

## Introduction

This playbook covers the step-by-step process of building and executing a cold outreach pipeline — from defining a precise ICP, to crafting messaging, designing sequences, and converting conversations into qualified opportunities.

---

## STEP 1: PROSPECTING

### 1.1 Define Your Real ICP

Your ICP (Ideal Customer Profile) is the most important decision you make in outbound. Get it wrong, and it doesn't matter how good your emails are or how confident you are on the phone — you're talking to the wrong people.

Most teams define their ICP based on company size, industry, and geography. That's a starting point, not a strategy. The best ICPs are built from the inside out — starting with your actual customers — and focus on problems, not profiles. Your ICP is ultimately a time management strategy: it determines where you spend your energy.

> (Source: John Barrows, LinkedIn post, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_makeithappen-activity-7427369592324526081-59ge, 02/2026)

---

#### How to Build a Real ICP

**Step 1 — Define ICP by problems, not appearance**

The most common mistake: targeting companies that *look* like your best customers. The right approach: targeting companies that have the *same problems* your best customers had before they bought from you.

Find the 5 reasons the problem was burning the hottest in your best accounts. Use those as your filters — not logo size or revenue range.

> (Source: Mark Kosoglow, "Pain Chain First, Outbound Second", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=86bzghP1ZJY , 30/05/2025)

---

**Step 2 — Audit your current customers**

Pull your full customer list and divide them into two groups:

- **Best customers:** Highest ACV, lowest churn, fastest sales cycle, fewest support tickets
- **Worst customers:** Lowest renewal rate, most support tickets, hardest to close, most likely to churn

Now look for patterns in each group. What do your best customers have in common that your worst customers don't?

---

**Step 3 — Go deeper than firmographics**

Industry and headcount are filters anyone can buy. Look at:

| Data Point | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Sub-industry | A healthcare SaaS and a logistics SaaS look the same on paper but buy differently |
| Size of the specific team you sell to | Company headcount is irrelevant — sales team size, finance team size, or IT team size matters |
| Tech stack | What tools they already use signals maturity and budget |
| Growth rate of the department | A team that just doubled headcount in 6 months is a different buyer than a stable team |
| Pain points mentioned before buying | The specific language customers used when describing their problem before they bought |

> (Source: Elric Legloire, ICP Complete Recipe PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Elric-Legloire/Elric-Post1.pdf , 03/26)

---

Your ICP definition should answer:

- What is the specific trigger that makes a company need us *right now*?
- What does the pain look like before they have us?
- Who specifically feels that pain every day?

---

### 1.2 Identify Buying Signals

Even within your ICP, not everyone is ready to buy right now. A buying signal tells you *when* to reach out — not just *who*. The same person at the same company can be a perfect prospect in March and a waste of your time in January, depending on what's happening in their world.

There are two types of signals: **public signals** (things you can find online) and **private signals** (things you can only learn through conversation).

---

#### Public Signals

**1. They viewed your LinkedIn profile**
Someone who looked at your profile has already shown interest. The window is short — reach out the same day.

How to use it: Open with: *"Saw you checked out my profile — what caught your eye?"* Simple, disarming, and gets a response because it's direct without being pushy.

---

**2. They follow your company page**
This person opted in to hearing from you. They made a conscious choice. They're not a cold stranger.

How to use it: Reference it directly: *"Noticed you follow [Company] — typically when people follow us they're working through [X, Y, or Z]. Is any of that on your plate?"*

---

**3. They posted on LinkedIn in the last 30 days**
Active users respond 3x more often than inactive ones. More importantly, you now have specific content to reference in your message.

How to use it: Comment on their post first (24–48 hours before messaging). When you do reach out, reference exactly what they said.

> (Source: Morgan J. Ingram, "5 Sales Navigator Filters That Actually Book Meetings", https://theampsocial.com/post/the-5-sales-navigator-filters-that-actually-book-meetings, April 2026)

---

**4. Hiring signals in job postings**
When a company posts a job with specific pain language in the description, the executive team has already made a funded decision. A VP of Sales role that mentions "building outbound from scratch" tells you exactly what they need.

How to use it: Use Claude or ChatGPT to scan job descriptions at target accounts. Ask: *"What pain does this job posting reveal? Who is the decision maker for solving this problem? Give me 3 outreach angles."*

> (Source: Morgan J. Ingram, LinkedIn post on AI prospecting workflow, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/morganjingramamp_this-ai-strategy-helped-me-book-5-target-activity-7449810720759787521-cXGx, 04/2026)

---

#### Private Signals

The most effective signal for reaching a senior executive can't be found on LinkedIn. It's inside information: what the executive is stressed about, what the team is struggling with, what this quarter's biggest pressure is.

**The groundswell play:**

1. Identify your target executive
2. Find 3–5 people on their team (junior, IC level)
3. Reach out with a low-friction ask — *"5 minutes — I have 2 questions about your [process]"*
4. Take what they share and open your executive message with it

**Example executive opener after groundswell:**
> *"I spoke with [Name] on your team. Here's what I'm hearing about your [forecasting/onboarding/pipeline] process. Here's how we typically help. Worth a 20-minute call?"*

This works because: (a) you come with real information instead of guesses, (b) name-dropping a team member adds instant credibility, and (c) almost nobody does it.

> (Source: Kyle Coleman, "Why Your Outbound Sucks and How to Fix It", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g9mejr_-odc, 16/08/2025)

## STEP 2: OUTREACH

### 2.1 Warm Up First

Most outbound pipelines go straight from list to meeting request. That's like walking up to a stranger and asking them to marry you. You haven't earned enough trust to make that ask.

The teams with consistently above-average reply rates spend time warming their prospects before any meeting CTA is sent. The key distinction: **cold outreach** means emailing strangers with no prior awareness. **Outbound** means contacting people who have already shown some signal of interest.

> (Source: Mark Kosoglow, LinkedIn post on cold vs warm outbound, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mkosoglow_outbound-is-hard-agree-but-it-can-be-easy-activity-7386412205967196160-VeLj, 12/2025)

---

#### How to Warm Up

**1. Comment on their LinkedIn posts**

Pick 5–10 people in your ICP who are active on LinkedIn. Leave a genuine 1–2 sentence comment on something they posted. Not "Great post!" — a real reaction or follow-up question.

Why it works: it notifies them of your existence, warms your future DMs and calls, and builds your presence in their network without a single pitch.

---

**2. Do groundswell work before messaging the decision maker**

Before messaging a VP or Director, connect with 3–5 people on their team using a blank connection request (blank requests have higher acceptance rates than personalised ones). This:

- Builds mutual connections with the DM (which increases their acceptance rate)
- Gives you real inside information to reference
- Makes your eventual message feel informed, not guessed

> (Source: Charly Johnson, 30MPC LinkedIn Prospect Guide PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Armand-Farrokh/30MPC%20Charly%20Johnsons%20LI%20Prospect%20Guide-1.pdf, 2024)

---

### 2.2 Cold Email

#### How to Write a Cold Email

**Start with the right mindset — lead with the problem, not the product.**

Gong analysed 100,000+ cold outreach interactions. Problem-based language: 16% success rate. Product buzzwords: 5.5%. Buyers don't care about your features until they recognise they have a problem. Lead with the problem. Let them ask what you do.

> (Source: Elric Legloire, "Stop Pitching Your Product on Cold Calls", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8OOVNEgkxRs, 03/02/2026)

---

**The skeleton — answer three questions:**

1. Who are you?
2. Why are you reaching out to this specific person?
3. What do you want?

**Format rules:**

- 5–6 sentences maximum
- Must fit on an iPhone screen without scrolling
- Assumptive language — not *"would you be open to"* but *"I'm looking to set some time"*
- Subject line: 3–5 words, lowercase, reads like a note from a peer

> (Source: Connor Murray, "15+ Years of No BS Cold Email Advice", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uvwej85d4Lo, 13/04/2026)

---

**What kills most emails:**

- First line starts with "I"
- Opens with *"I love what you're doing at [Company]"* — no one believes this
- Pitches the product before the problem is established
- Asks for 30 minutes before earning 30 seconds of trust

**The fix:** the first line is always about them — something about their company, their role, or something they said. Never about you.

> (Source: Tom Slocum, LinkedIn post on Inbox Theater, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/tomslocum_most-sales-teams-think-cold-email-is-broken-activity-7386065013553418240-FSEl, 12/2025)

---

#### Email Sequence Cadence

Don't drag sequences for months. Most replies come in the first 5 touches. A tight sequence of 4–5 emails over 7–10 business days outperforms a 15-touch sequence spread over 60 days.

> (Source: Connor Murray, "15+ Years of No BS Cold Email Advice", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uvwej85d4Lo, 13/04/2026)*Source: Connor Murray, "15+ Years of No BS Cold Email Advice," Apr 2026*

| Touch | Timing | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Email 1 | Day 1 | Core value message — who, why, what |
| Email 2 | Day 2–3 | *"Just want to make sure you caught my note — do either of those times work?"* |
| Email 3 | Day 5 | *"Please give me your thoughts on this."* One sentence. Nothing else. |
| Email 4 | Day 7–8 | Different angle — try a different problem or trigger |
| Email 5 (Breakup) | Day 10 | *"[First name], haven't heard back since [date]. Did I miss a step somewhere? — [Your name]"* |

The breakup email (Email 5) averages a **56% response rate** when written this simply.

> (Source: Vin Matano, 30MPC "5 Emails from Prospecting to Close" PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Armand-Farrokh/Vin%20Matano-5%20Emails%20from%20Prospecting%20to%20Close.pdf , 2024)

---

#### Cross-Channel Follow-Up

If a prospect opens your email 3+ times without replying, they're interested but distracted. Send a LinkedIn InMail referencing the subject line: *"Hey [Name], did you see my email on [subject line]? Worth a look."*

This works because it's unexpected, it nudges them back to a message they already engaged with, and it feels human — not automated.

> (Source: Morgan J. Ingram, LinkedIn post on cross-channel tactic, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/morganjingramamp_i-told-people-to-never-do-this-outbound-tactic-activity-7432413739112595456-AIK1, 03/2026)

---

#### Exception: C-Suite With Deep Research

For C-suite prospects where you've done proper strategic research and can connect your message to a specific initiative (IPO, international expansion, M&A), a longer email of 150–200 words is acceptable. The rule is: **length is earned by value.** Every sentence must earn the next.

> (Source: Kyle Coleman, "How to Use AI for Prospecting Executives", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7OdRu6_YqGc, 2024)

---

### 2.3 Cold Call

Cold calling is the fastest feedback loop in sales. In 10 minutes you learn more about a prospect than you would from 20 emails going unanswered. The problem isn't cold calling — it's cold calling without preparation or a clear framework.

---

#### Before You Dial — Verify the Right Person

Approximately 60% of cold calls are pitched to the wrong person. Before optimising any part of your script, confirm the person on the list is actually the decision maker or influencer for your solution.

A simple verification pass — checking LinkedIn to confirm title and responsibilities — eliminates the biggest wasted effort in cold calling.

> (Source: Josh Braun, LinkedIn post on wrong person problem, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_when-i-listen-to-cold-calls-around-60-of-activity-7449132882620690432-Ie-p, 04/2026)

---

#### The Value Statement Framework

In the first 30 seconds of a cold call, a prospect is unconsciously asking three questions: *Who is this? Do I know them? Should we continue this conversation?* Your job is to answer all three before they even think to hang up.

>(Source: Tom Slocum, "From Cold to Warm Calling", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3y1OkWjaevE , 16/08/22)

**Example script:**

**Step 1 — Intro + why this person** *(10 sec)*
> *"Hey [Name], this is [Your Name] from [Company]. I'm calling because I've been talking to a lot of [titles] like you — and one thing keeps coming up."*

**Step 2 — Illuminate the problem** *(15 sec)*
> *"Most [titles] I speak to don't realise that [specific blind spot or hidden cost]. It usually doesn't show up until [consequence]."*

**Step 3 — Let them react** *(question, not pitch)*
> *"I wasn't sure if that's something showing up on your end or not."*

**Step 4 — Meeting ask only after they engage**
> *"That's exactly why I called — worth 20 minutes to show you how we've helped [similar teams] fix that? I'm free Thursday or Friday morning."*

> (Source: Steps 1 and 4 — Connor Murray, "How to Be a Top Performing SDR/BDR", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nlSOPrYjKzs, 06/04/2026. Steps 2 and 3 — Josh Braun, "How to Make a Cold Call", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hpXev0vUcvQ, 26/032026)


---

#### Tone Is the Real Script

The words matter less than most people think. Your tone — how confident, calm, and genuine you sound — determines whether the prospect keeps listening.

**Practical tips:**

- **Stand up when dialing.** It opens your diaphragm, improves your vocal energy, and makes you sound more confident. *(Source: Armand Farrokh, LinkedIn post on tone, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/armand-farrokh_on-a-cold-call-your-tone-matters-far-more-activity-7432852163648720896-pah1, 03/2026)*
- **Breathe between dials.** Take 2–3 breaths as the phone is ringing on every call. *(Source: Tom Slocum, "From Cold to Warm Calling", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3y1OkWjaevE , 16/08/22)*
- **Detach from the outcome.** 46 dials reliably produces 2–3 good conversations. Know your ratio. Rejection stops feeling personal when you understand the math. *(Source: Josh Braun, "How to Be Relaxed and Chill Cold Calling", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jmkjpDaic2o, 22/03/2026)*

The goal of the cold call is not to close the deal. It's to get the right person to accept a 20–30 minute calendar invite. That's a much easier sell.

---

#### Block Dedicated Call Time

Cold calling done randomly is cold calling done poorly. Block two dedicated 1-hour calling sessions per day — one in the **8–9 AM** window, one in the **4–5 PM** window. These are when people are generally not in meetings. Protect these like you would protect a client call.

> (Source: Connor Murray, "How to Be a Top Performing SDR/BDR", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nlSOPrYjKzs, 06/04/2026)

---

### 2.4 Objection Handling

An objection is not a rejection. It's almost always either a defence reflex or a sign that you haven't yet established enough value or trust. The best reps treat every objection as information, not as a door closing.

---

#### "Not Interested" — The Most Common Swat

*"Not interested"* is not a real objection. It's a swat — an automatic defence. The real objection is hiding underneath it. Your job is to release the pressure first, then find what's actually driving it.

**Three-step response:**

1. **Agree:** *"Totally fair — you've got no reason to be."* (This surprises them. They expected pushback.)
2. **Incentivise:** *"Before I let you go — is it that the timing is off, or is this genuinely not a priority right now?"*
3. **Handle the real objection** once it surfaces

> (Source: Armand Farrokh, LinkedIn post on "not interested", https://www.linkedin.com/posts/armand-farrokh_not-interested-is-the-1-objection-on-a-cold-activity-7443350253627572224-6WB9, 03/2026)

---

#### The Defuse → Discover → Discuss Framework

For every common objection, build a prepared 3-step response:

| Step | Purpose | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Defuse | Remove tension, signal you heard them | *"I get it." / "Fair point."* |
| Discover | Find what's actually driving the objection | *"Is it that the timing is off, or is this not on your radar at all?"* |
| Discuss | A path forward that adds value | *"The reason I ask is [brief insight]. Worth 15 minutes to see if this is relevant?"* |

> (Source: Josh Braun, Objection Flashcards PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Josh-Braun/30MPC-09-Sep2023-Josh%20Braun%20Objection%20Flashcards_V2.pdf , 09/2023)

**Build your flashcard library.** Write down your top 5–10 objections. Write a Defuse → Discover → Discuss response for each one. Practice until the response is instant.

## STEP 3: THE MEETING

### 3.1 Pre-Call Research and Preparation

There's a direct relationship between how prepared you are before a call and how well the call goes. Preparation isn't just about knowing their company name and LinkedIn headline — it's about forming a real point of view before you get on the call.

---

#### Pre-Call Research Checklist

Run through this before every discovery call or demo:

- **Company website:** What are they selling, who to, how do they position themselves?
- **Press room / recent news:** Any announcements, funding rounds, new product launches, leadership changes?
- **Job postings:** What functions are they investing in? What pain does the hiring reveal?
- **Your contact's LinkedIn:** Recent posts, comments, articles — what are they thinking about?
- **Their customers:** Who do they sell to? This shapes how they think about investment decisions.
- **Competitors:** Who else are they likely evaluating? What are the common objections in this space?
- **If public:** Skim the latest 10-K or earnings call for stated priorities.

> (Source: Morgan J. Ingram, AMP x HubSpot Playbook PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/MorganJIngram/AMP-Hubspot-Playbook-2024.pdf , 2024)

> (Source: Kyle Coleman, LinkedIn post on pre-call research, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kyletcoleman_your-prospect-googled-you-before-the-call-activity-7428140069116469249-eMV4, 02/2026)

---

#### Inbound Hand-Raises Still Need a Human Touch

When someone books a demo through your website, 20–30% will no-show if no human has touched the conversation before the call. A booked calendar event is not a commitment — it's an intention.

After someone books, send a one-line personalised message confirming you're looking forward to it. That single human interaction significantly improves show rates.

> (Source: Kyle Coleman, LinkedIn post on inbound no-shows, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/kyletcoleman_someone-clicked-book-a-demo-on-your-website-activity-7429218250007195648-4OWE, 02/2026)


---

### 3.2 Discovery and Demo

#### Come In With a Hypothesis

Don't open a discovery call with *"Tell me about your business."* That signals you did no preparation. It also puts the burden entirely on the prospect to do your job for you.

Instead, form a hypothesis from your research and open with it. This shows preparation, creates direction, and gives the prospect something to react to — which immediately turns your call into a conversation.

**Hypothesis-led opening:**
> *"Based on what I've been seeing with [type of company / industry], the thing that tends to cause the most friction is [specific challenge] — usually because [brief reason]. I wanted to check if that's showing up for your team, or if something else is higher on the priority list."*

> (Source: John Barrows, LinkedIn post on hypothesis-led discovery, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_i-dont-do-discovery-at-least-not-the-activity-7429268405381242880-UWI3, 02/2026)

---

#### Run Discovery as a Funnel, Not a List of Questions

Discovery is not an interrogation. It's a funnel — you start broad and get progressively more specific. Each answer earns the next question. Don't jump to quantifying the problem before you understand what the problem is.

| Level | Question Type | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Broad | Current state | *"What does your [process] look like today, end to end?"* |
| Focus | Where it breaks | *"Where does it tend to slow down or create friction?"* |
| Quantify | Cost of the problem | *"How much time does that cost your team per [week/quarter]?"* |
| Project | Ideal state | *"In an ideal world, what would that look like instead?"* |
| Validate | Success criteria | *"If we solve this, what does a good outcome look like to you?"* |

> (Source: Connor Murray, "How Top AEs Run Discovery Calls", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4tzjLND4IxY, 23/032026)

---

#### Run Demos That Engage — Not Demos That Inform

Most demos are a mistake. The rep shares their screen and walks through features while the prospect nods along, remembers 20% of what they saw, and asks for more time to think about it.

The problem: you're informing them, not involving them.

**Before you share your screen:**

Go around the room (or the Zoom call) and ask each person one question: *"What's the one thing you need to see today to feel comfortable with the next step?"* Write it down. Then demo in that order.

> (Source: John Barrows, "How to Run an Effective Demo", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1agPDnTmHiw, 17/04/2026)

Replace *"Does that make sense?"* — which no one ever answers honestly — with:
> *"You mentioned [their stated priority] was important. How does what you just saw compare to how you're handling it today?"*

**Demo rules:**

- Keep it under 18 minutes. Teams that do this cut deal cycles by 25%.
- Personalise to their industry. One relevant case study beats 101 logos.
- Make them interact. Memory retention for things they *did*: 90%+. Things they *watched*: 65–80%. Things they only *heard*: 10–20%.

> (Source: Mark Kosoglow, LinkedIn post on demo retention science, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mkosoglow_i-reviewed-100-sales-decks-last-quarter-activity-7407057064495058944-kcxA, 01/2026)

### 3.3 Closing and Negotiation

#### Build a Business Case

**Armand Farrokh's three-part structure:**

**Part 1 — Problems + Solution**
Recap the qualitative business drivers the buyer told you they care about. Use their exact language.

**Part 2 — Quantifiable Impact**
Run a working session with your champion. Build the numbers together, line by line. When the inputs come from them, the ROI is defensible to their CFO because they built it.

**Part 3 — Unquantifiable Impact**
The emotional cost that can't go in a spreadsheet — but is often what actually gets the deal bought internally.

> (Source: Armand Farrokh, 30MPC x Responsive Proposal Accelerators PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Armand-Farrokh/Responsive_3%20Proposal%20Accelerators%20That%20Close%20Deals%20Faster%20(And%20Protect%20Your%20ACV).pdf , 2026)

---

#### Run Procurement, Security, and Legal in Parallel

After the verbal from their champion, most reps run vendor review one workstream at a time — two weeks in procurement, two weeks in security, two weeks in legal. And just like that, the EOM date slips.

Top reps ask to run procurement, security, and legal **in parallel** the moment they get the verbal. As soon as you have the green light, share the right resources with your champion:

- **Procurement:** The proposal and business case you've already aligned on
- **Security:** Your SOC II, privacy policy, and any docs they'll request under NDA
- **Legal:** Your order form and MSA so terms can be reviewed immediately

Then have your champion spin up separate threads for each team.

> (Source: Armand Farrokh, 30MPC x Responsive Proposal Accelerators PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Armand-Farrokh/Responsive_3%20Proposal%20Accelerators%20That%20Close%20Deals%20Faster%20(And%20Protect%20Your%20ACV).pdf , 2026)

---

#### Finish Negotiation in One Cut

Most sellers treat negotiation like a free handout. The prospect asks for something and they give — first price, then extra seats, next billing frequency, and finally net-90 terms. Each concession shrinks your commission and drags out the deal.

Top sellers don't negotiate in instalments. They get the full and final ask upfront.

**Armand Farrokh's talk track:**
> *"Jane, I get that price matters. But if I go back to my CFO for this discount, they're going to ask me one thing: 'Is this what actually gets the deal done — or are billing terms and cash flow still on the table?'"*

Surface everything they need to sign, force real tradeoffs, and close in one cut.

> (Source: Armand Farrokh, 30MPC x Responsive Proposal Accelerators PDF, https://github.com/aputria/100hires/blob/main/research/other/pdf/Armand-Farrokh/Responsive_3%20Proposal%20Accelerators%20That%20Close%20Deals%20Faster%20(And%20Protect%20Your%20ACV).pdf , 2026)

## EDITORIAL SECTIONS

### Where Experts Disagree

---

**Disagreement 1: How to Open a Discovery Call**

**John Barrows** recommends opening with a hypothesis. Come in with a point of view based on research — *"Based on what I'm seeing with companies like yours, the friction tends to be X. Is that showing up for your team?"* Credibility is earned through the quality of your hypothesis, not stated explicitly. *(Source: John Barrows, LinkedIn post on hypothesis-led discovery, https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_i-dont-do-discovery-at-least-not-the-activity-7429268405381242880-UWI3, 02/2026)*

**Connor Murray** recommends opening with a credibility statement. Establish who you are, who you work with, and what outcomes you drive — *"We work with regional banks typically dealing with X and Y..."* — before handing it back to the prospect. *(Source: Connor Murray, "How Top AEs Run Discovery Calls", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4tzjLND4IxY, 23/03/2026)*

**My take:** I side with Barrows. The credibility statement *tells* the prospect you're prepared. The hypothesis *shows* them you're prepared. Starting with their world instead of yours signals that the call is about them, not about you. The only caveat: a weak or generic hypothesis is worse than a credibility statement, so this approach only works if you've done real pre-call research.

---

**Disagreement 2: Permission-Based Openers on Cold Calls**

**Tom Slocum** recommends against them. If the prospect picked up the phone, they've already given permission. Asking for it again wastes the moment you just earned. His exact reasoning: *"the first three things that you're trying to do in that 30 seconds with this call is who is it, how do I know them, and should we continue this conversation — so just get into it."* *(Source: Tom Slocum, "From Cold to Warm Calling", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3y1OkWjaevE , 16/08/22)*

**Kyle Coleman** uses them confidently. His opener: *"This is a cold call. Do you want to hang up or do you want to roll the dice?"* He calls it cheeky, acknowledges it requires confidence, and says it consistently earns him 30 seconds. *(Source: Kyle Coleman, "Why Your Outbound Sucks and How to Fix It", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=g9mejr_-odc, 16/08/25)*

**My take:** I side with Tom Slocum. A cheeky opener like this is a gamble. For an experienced rep with natural confidence, it may work. For everyone else, any hesitation in the voice turns it into an awkward exchange that's harder to recover from than a standard opener. The value statement is safer, more consistent, and works across skill levels.

---

**Disagreement 3: Speed vs. Research Before Outreach**

**Connor Murray** recommends moving fast. The biggest mistake SDRs make is sitting on the perfection side of the scale — deep research, heavy personalization, too much time per account. If your KPI is 15–20 meetings a month, that approach is too slow. The fix is persona-based templates by industry or title that let you move fast while still sounding relevant. *"You end up spending 15 minutes researching someone who might not even respond."* *(Source: Connor Murray, "How to Be a Top Performing SDR/BDR", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=nlSOPrYjKzs , 06/04/2026)*

**Tom Slocum** recommends knowing the story on every person before you dial. Not deep research — just genuine familiarity with who you're calling and why. *"If you generally have 50 cold calls to make in a day and you know the story on all those 50 people, you have nothing to be afraid of. Now when you call 100 strangers, that's scary."* *(Source: Tom Slocum, "From Cold to Warm Calling", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3y1OkWjaevE , 16/08/22)*

**My take:** I side with Connor for email, and Tom for cold calls. Email at volume requires systems and templates — individual research per prospect doesn't scale. But cold calling without knowing who you're calling is exactly why most reps sound like they're reading to a stranger. The research standard should follow the channel.

### What I Rejected and Why

---

**1. Pain Chain Email Sequence** *(Mark Kosoglow)*

What it is: Send 5–8 educational emails addressing connected pain points before ever asking for a meeting. No meeting CTA in any of them. The goal is to move the buyer from unaware to aware. Only ask for the meeting after they engage.

Why rejected: 5–8 emails before asking for a meeting is too many. It sounds like a drip campaign. The prospect will assume you're running automation and tune out. If you're an early-stage rep with 100 accounts, you don't have time to send 8 emails per prospect before asking. This may work at scale with content marketing support; it doesn't work for basic cold outreach.

*Source: Mark Kosoglow, "Pain Chain First, Outbound Second", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=86bzghP1ZJY, 30/05/2025*

---

**2. The 5C Social Selling System** *(Morgan J. Ingram)*

What it is: A complete social selling framework with five steps — Clarity, Connect, Conversation, Comment, Content — covering Sales Navigator filters, LinkedIn profile optimization, outreach via video/voice notes, daily commenting discipline, and weekly content curation.

Why rejected: This is a full-time social selling motion that requires daily LinkedIn activity, profile optimisation, video recording, commenting discipline, and content curation. For a rep doing basic cold outreach (email + calls), adding this entire system on top of their existing workload is overwhelming. It's not that it doesn't work — it's that it's a separate channel strategy that belongs in a social selling playbook. The playbook already covers LinkedIn research and personalisation. The 5C system is overkill for someone just trying to write better cold emails and make better cold calls.

*Source: Morgan J. Ingram, "The 5C Social Selling System That Fixes Broken Outbound", https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-1GgA8LW4ks, 16/04/26*

### My Original Ideas

---

**Idea 1: Find Your Competitor's Unhappy Customers**

Most reps spend their time trying to convince people they have a problem. This idea skips that entirely.

Go to G2, Capterra, or Trustpilot and read the 1–3 star reviews left for your direct competitors. These aren't cold prospects. These are people who already recognised they had a problem, already went through the effort of finding a solution, already paid for it — and it still didn't work. They're not unaware. They're disappointed. And disappointed people are actively looking for something better.

Find the reviewer on LinkedIn. Read what they complained about specifically — slow onboarding, missing features, clunky UI, poor support, doesn't do what it promised. That complaint is your opener.

Not: *"Hi, I saw you might be struggling with hiring automation."*
But: *"Saw your review of [Competitor] — sounds like the automation didn't hold up when volume picked up. That's exactly the gap we built our product around."*

You're not pitching. You're answering a question they already asked out loud. The review is the brief. Your product is the answer.

---

**Idea 2: The Three-Tier Prospect List**

Split your prospect list into three tiers based on how many active signals a person is showing — not just whether they fit your ICP.

>**Tier 1 — Hot:** 2+ active signals (viewed your profile AND recently changed jobs, or posted AND follows your company). These people get deep personalisation, and you hit all three channels — call, email, LinkedIn — in the same week.

>**Tier 2 — Warm:** One strong signal (recent post, follows your company, viewed your profile once). These get a quality-at-scale template adapted specifically to their trigger — not fully personalised, but not generic either.

>**Tier 3 — Cold ICP match:** Fits your ICP criteria but showing no active signals. These get high-volume, persona-targeted templates. You reach out because they fit, not because they're ready.

This framework forces you to separate the two decisions instead of collapsing them into one list. The signal filters come from Morgan J. Ingram's Sales Navigator breakdown. The tiering structure and decision to treat them as separate prioritisation layers is my own suggestion.

### Weaknesses of This Playbook

1. **It's a general playbook.** This playbook covers cold outreach broadly. It doesn't account for industry type, company size, or what you're selling. A one-person SaaS startup selling to SMBs operates completely differently from an enterprise team selling six-figure deals to Fortune 500s.

2. **It's built on the most recent content, not foundational work.** Sources mostly come from the most recent posts, videos, and documents each expert published at the time of research. It captures what these experts are saying right now but may miss the deeper frameworks they built their reputation on.

3. **The prospecting section assumes you already have customers.** The ICP-building framework is built around pulling patterns from your existing customer base. If you're at an early stage with fewer than 20 customers, that data doesn't exist yet.

4. **It assumes deals close faster than they actually do.** The pipeline section is written as if a deal can move from discovery to close in a handful of touchpoints. In reality, most B2B deals take multiple meetings, internal approvals, budget conversations, and follow-ups that stretch over weeks or months.

5. **It doesn't cover what happens when the basics stop working.** This playbook assumes your emails land in inboxes, your calls connect, and your LinkedIn account stays active. It doesn't cover what to do when your domain gets flagged as spam, your open rates drop to zero, or your LinkedIn account gets restricted for too much outreach activity.

### Who I Would NOT Recommend Following and Why

Every expert I researched contributed something that made it into this playbook, so this section is less about who to avoid and more about how to use their advice wisely.

Most of the ten fall into two camps. **Tom Slocum, Morgan J. Ingram, John Barrows, and Josh Braun** are experienced practitioners who have since moved into coaching and content creation. They're still actively updating their thinking, especially around AI in sales, and some of those ideas are genuinely worth applying. Whether all of it holds up in the current market is something only testing will tell.

**Kyle Coleman and Connor Murray** are still inside companies, building pipelines in real time. Their content reflects what's working right now, though it's worth noting it may lean toward their own company's context and product — which is fine, as long as you account for it.

The difference matters when deciding how much weight to give any single piece of advice. What worked in a different market, era, or company size needs to be tested. Take what makes logical sense for your specific context, test it, and cut what doesn't work.
