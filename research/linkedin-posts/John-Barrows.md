## Post 1
- **Date:** February 2026
- **Engagement:** 130 likes · 29 comments · 1 repost
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_i-dont-do-discovery-at-least-not-the-activity-7429268405381242880-UWI3)
- **Why it matters:** Reframes discovery as hypothesis-led research rather than lazy open-ended questioning

I don't "do discovery."

At least not the way most reps do it.

When I walk into a prospect meeting, I don't start with:
 - "Tell me about your business."
 - "What are your priorities?"
 - "What keeps you up at night?"

That's lazy.

I do the homework first by researching the industry and company, look for trigger events, study hiring trends, analyzing messaging and more. 

I do all this so I can develop a hypothesis on what the client's potential challenges are and how I might be able to help and open the conversation with something like this: 

"Based on what I'm seeing, companies like yours are struggling with X because of Y. Is that happening here?"

That anchor changes the conversation.

It shows preparation, creates direction and gives the client something to react to. 

And here's the key. A hypothesis is not a conclusion. It's a starting point. You still have to listen.

That's why I loved this recent conversation with Saif Khan from Semrush on this week's episode of the Make It Happen Monday podcast.

We talked about how managers should use the same approach in performance reviews.

Too many managers show up with no point of view.

Instead, they should come in with a hypothesis:
 - "Your close rate is strong, but pipeline coverage is light."
 - "Your prep is solid, but late-stage control slips."

Then say:
 - "Here's what I'm seeing. Where am I wrong?"

That's leadership.

Do the work. Form a point of view. Lead with it. Then shut up and listen.

Do you walk into conversations with a hypothesis, or start from scratch every time?

## Post 2
- **Date:** February 2026
- **Engagement:** 82 likes · 24 comments · 1 repost
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_makeithappen-activity-7427369592324526081-59ge)
- **Why it matters:** Goes deeper than standard ICP criteria — introduces the concept of your ICP's ICP as a targeting strategy

Your ICP probably sucks.

Not because you're bad at sales.

Because you stopped at "industry, revenue, employee count."

Over the past few months I've done a ton of trainings.

Before every session I ask for the prep docs. And I always ask for their Ideal Customer Profile. Preferably broken down by Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3.

Here's what I usually get:
- Industry
- Company size
- Revenue range
- Geography

That's it.

That's not an ICP. That's a basic filtered list that any data provider can give you.

And right now, where quality beats quantity every time, where you spend your time matters more than ever.

Your ICP is a time management strategy.

Let me give you a real example.

In early 2023, 80% of my clients were in the SaaS/Tech and had money in Silicon Valley Bank when everything collapsed.

When tech froze, my business froze.

I had a choice:
1) Panic and spray outreach everywhere
2) Revisit my ICP like a scientist

I chose option 2.
But I didn't just look at my Ideal Customer Profile.

I looked at my ICP's ICP.

If they were in SaaS AND most of their customers were in SaaS, they were getting crushed just like me.

BUT, If they were in SaaS but sold into retail, manufacturing, healthcare, industries still spending, they were fine.
 
Same company size, titles and tech stack but a completely different buying behavior.

That one shift in focus changed my results and got me back on track.

Most companies never go that deep.

They don't look at:
- Department size, not company size
- Growth rate of the specific function
- Tech stack maturity
- Competitive contracts in place
- Average deal size of the accounts they sell to
- Who their customers sell to

And then we wonder why conversion rates are down.

If you're struggling with pipeline right now, don't immediately blame messaging.

Revisit your ICP.

Be ruthless about it.

Because your Tier 1 accounts deserve a completely different strategy than your Tier 3s.

- **Engagement:** 80 likes · 22 comments · 1 repost
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/johnbarrows_win-the-right-way-sometimes-in-business-activity-7404187218539466752-sa98)
- **Why it matters:** Honest take on the danger of chasing bad-fit revenue — a trap most early-stage sales teams fall into

"Win the right way"

Sometimes in business and in Sales it's really tempting to try to "win" revenue or customers that aren't the right fit for your business.

It's usually because you NEED the revenue.

The problem is that when you take on bad revenue or bad customers, it kills your ability to grow.

Every start-up and small business I know goes through this process. 

They tend to bring on customers early that aren't a great fit but they pay the bills. 

Then, eventually they have to move more towards the type of revenue and customers that are the right fit and more sustainable.

Sometimes (most of the time) they have to "fire" some of the early customers that got them to where they are.

This is not a fun process to go through but it's necessary. 

I got into this topic and much more with Rex Galbraith from Consensus on this week's episode of the Make it Happen Monday podcast.

He talked about a lot of the success and failures of growing the business along the way and Consensus' "Win the right way" mentality.

Anyone who is starting or growing a business should listen to this one. 

What's an example of a customer or revenue that you brought on that wasn't the right fit and how did it impact your business and ability to grow?

## Post 4
- **Date:** January 2026
- **Engagement:** 72 likes · 28 comments · 2 reposts

- **Why it matters:** Challenges reps to develop real business acumen — not just use trigger events as a surface tactic

Everyone talks about finding and using "trigger events" to personalize their prospecting efforts but does anyone really understand the "why" behind the trigger event?

Most don't. 

For instance, an M&A is a trigger event that many reps use but most of them have never been through an M&A in a leadership role and have no idea the phases businesses go through and what they need at each step. 

That's why their messaging falls flat. 

They don't have the business acumen to make the real/right connection. 

That's why I tell anyone who is looking to improve their sales results to improve their business acumen over any technique or tip I could ever teach them. 
I sat down to discuss this and a lot more with Lorenzo Bizzi on this week's episode of the Make it Happen Monday podcast. 

Lorenzo is an author, professor, and real-world business strategist who grew up in Italy, studied business at Bocconi University in Milan, and fell in love with blending theory and practice. 

He's now based on based in Los Angeles and teaches strategy at Cal State and trains CEOs across industries.

This conversation goes deep on one major idea: Sales is applied strategy.

Most reps are taught tactics, scripts, subject lines, cadences, etc but they miss the big picture: how business works. 

And without business acumen, tactics fall flat. Lorenzo argues that if you want to be taken seriously, you don't just need to sound credible. You need to be credible.

- **Engagement:** 132 likes · 43 comments · 3 reposts

- **Why it matters:** Calls out the broken buyer experience created by Predictable Revenue and makes the case for buyer-centric selling

"Why have we made it this hard?"
"Why have we not thought about the buyer and put them at the center of the experience?"

I've said it before and I'll say it again. Predictable Revenue (segmentation of roles) WAS great for us as companies to scale our sales teams but it was NEVER a customer centric way of selling?

This is what I dove into with 🎺 Rory Sadler on this week's episode of the Make it Happen Monday podcast. 

Buyers have all the power now. The sales process? Full of friction, too many handoffs, repeated and/or irrelevant questions and almost no personalization.

And guess what's killing deals? Indecision. 

Not price. Not competition. Just an overwhelmed buyer who doesn't have time to figure out your product or how to sell it internally.

Here's what's shifting fast:
 - The old playbook (predictable revenue, handoffs, stages) is crumbling.
 - Buyers want answers now, not a meeting in two weeks.
 - Sales reps need to bring value every single interaction.
 - Mutual action plans and buyer enablement aren't "nice to have." They're deal savers.
 - Reps who don't prep, personalize, and guide the buyer? You're irrelevant.

It's not about what you sell anymore. It's how you sell. The experience is the differentiator.

This conversation with Rory breaks down how sales needs to evolve and how reps can step up and win by making the process feel easy, personalized, and valuable.

Are you selling like it's 2025 or still running the 2010 playbook?
