## Post 1

- **Engagement:** 39 likes · 10 comments · 3 reposts
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_anxiety-in-sales-doesnt-come-from-the-call-activity-7450883459784425472-M3NB)
- **Why it matters:** Addresses the root cause of sales anxiety — a psychological reframe that directly impacts cold call performance

Anxiety in sales doesn't come from the call.
Or the prospect.
Or the objection.

It comes from the gap between what's actually happening
and what you thought was supposed to happen.

You expected the prospect to be nice.
They were short.

You expected the meeting to be smooth.
It veered off the rails.

You expected a yes or a no. 
You got a maybe.

The bigger that gap, the bigger the knot in your chest.

The fix isn't trying to control the outcome.
You can't. 
People buy on their timelines and for their reasons not yours. 

The fix is let go of having expectations.

Because the moment you stop demanding the moment be different,
the moment stops feeling like a threat.

And that's when the anxiety finally lets go.

Do your best, forget the rest.

## Post 3
- **Date:** April 2026
- **Engagement:** 21 likes · 9 comments
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_i-hate-to-bother-you-if-this-isnt-relevant-activity-7450636878745300992-Y4mj)
- **Why it matters:** Exposes how apologetic language unconsciously signals insecurity — and how to enter conversations with calm confidence instead

"I hate to bother you if this isn't relevant…"

It sounds polite.
But underneath it is something else.

A subtle apology for existing.

When you lead with "sorry to bother you," you're quietly saying:
"I might not belong here."
"I might be an interruption."
"I might be wasting your time."

That tension is felt before the conversation even starts.

Your energy arrives before your words.

If you show up unsure, the other person has to manage that uncertainty.
They brace.
They protect.
They disengage.

But notice what your role is.

You're not knocking on doors selling encyclopedias.
You're not asking for a favor.
You're doing work that surfaces friction people might be living with.

That isn't a bother.
It's often a relief.

So instead of apologizing for the conversation, just enter it.

Calmly.
Detached.
Neutrally.
Curious. 
Without an agenda.

"When calls come in after hours, are you using an answering service, automated text messages saying you'll follow up in the morning, or an AI dispatcher that takes the call and books the appointment?"

You're not an interruption.
You're an invitation.

## Post 4
- **Date:** April 2026
- **Engagement:** 27 likes · 16 comments
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_good-cold-emails-create-a-question-the-mind-activity-7449884988268883968-wdiJ)
- **Why it matters:** Shows how to use curiosity gaps and blind spots to make prospects lean in — psychology applied directly to cold email writing

Good cold emails create a question the mind wants answered.

Take this line:

"How are you ensuring your car wash mitt won't scratch your Audi S4?"

Now the mind pauses.

"Wait… that's a thing? Better keep reading."

That pause is curiosity.

Great cold emails don't start with what you sell.

Why?

What you sell has no value without a problem.

Good cold emails start with illuminating a blind spot.

A hidden cost.
An unseen downside.
A risk of doing nothing.

The moment someone thinks:

"Hold on… I might be missing something…"

they lean forward.

If there's no tension, there's no attention.

## Post 5
- **Date:** April 2026
- **Engagement:** 49 likes · 25 comments · 1 repost
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/josh-braun_when-i-listen-to-cold-calls-around-60-of-activity-7449132882620690432-Ie-p)
- **Why it matters:** Simple but often overlooked fix — verify you're talking to the right person before optimizing anything else in your cold call process

When I listen to cold calls, around 60% of the time the rep isn't speaking to the right person.

So the conversation fizzles.

Not because the script is bad. 

They're simply talking to someone who has nothing to do with what they're saying. 

Let's fix that.

When I managed a team of SDRs, we did something simple before the reps ever picked up the phone.

We had a freelancer call down the list to make sure we had the right person first.

No pitch.

No meeting ask.

Just orientation.

The script:

"Hi Jane, this is David with Jellyvision.
We've never spoken, but I was hoping you could help me out for a moment.

I'm looking to send some information to the person responsible for benefits communication.
Would that be Melissa Davis?"

That's it.

Now when the SDR called, they knew they were speaking to someone for whom the conversation actually mattered.

Before fixing the script, fix who's hearing it.
