- **Why it matters:** Data-backed breakdown of what separates top performers (72% win rate) from average ones (21%) in how they use sales decks

I reviewed 100 sales decks last quarter.

3 were good.

The rest? They were killing deals and the reps had no idea.

I just read a report analyzing 10,000+ B2B sales decks and it confirmed what I've always known...

Average performers win 21% of deals. Top performers win 72%.

Same product. Same market. Same buyer.

Here are 3 insights from the research that stood out:

1️⃣ Personalized decks convert 28% higher.

But most AE's think slapping a company name on Slide 1 is personalization. 
It's not.

Real personalization means your deck reflects what they told you in discovery - their pain, their use cases, what they're trying to achieve.

If you're not mapping your deck to their specific situation, you're just using a template with their logo on it.

This is also a sign that you're not running effective discovery or you're ignoring what buyers told you.

2️⃣ Relevant case studies boost trust by 40%

Your customer logos don't work the way you think..

101 logos on a slide doesn't build credibility. 

What's more valuable? 101 shiny logos or one story where you solved the exact problem your prospect is facing for someone in their exact industry?

That's social proof that matters.

3️⃣ Teams that keep decks under 18 minutes cut deal cycles by 25%.

Your decks are way too long.

TED Talks can't go longer than 18 minutes for a reason.

But, you think your 30+ minute demo wins deals. Wrong.

We start to tune out after 10 minutes.

Unless, they are re-engaged (story, pace shift, surprise).

That means your buyer is tuned out by minute 20 and you're still presenting for 10 more minutes to someone who stopped listening.

The research DIGIDECK produced has data insights and frameworks you can implement immediately.

## Post 2
- **Date:** December 2025
- **Engagement:** 59 likes · 12 comments
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mkosoglow_if-you-dont-have-sales-marketing-alignment-activity-7392195311957331968-WdVh)
- **Why it matters:** Uses brain science to show why interactive demos create 90% memory retention vs 10% for verbal-only pitches — a compelling case for buyer-seller alignment

If you don't have Sales-Marketing alignment, which is literally a simple choice to collaborate with peers for a shared goal to grow (c'mon y'all - why is this a thing??!!), you def don't have an even more powerful alignment.

Buyer-Seller alignment is the real unlock.

Choosing to ignore marketing asks of you that could generate pipeline is nuts.

Choosing to ignore buyer asks of you that could win deals is nuts-ier.



Here's a truth about buyers.

Buyers in a deal think about the decision significantly less than Sellers in a deal.

Buyers don't want to do the work of the evaluation (most sellers don't either - yikes!).

They have 10-20 things they are working so you get max 5-10% of their attention.



There is one way that ALWAYS gets you more time.

Creating trust.

When buyers trust sellers, they give out-sized time and attention.

But, trust is harder than ever to create.

There are more vendors, more slick slide decks, more niche solutions, more of "the same." It's confusing.

To create trust, you need to be remembered.

To be remembered, you have to make it easy to be remembered.

You can do this in a lot of ways:
- injecting your personality
- be more transparent about who you are as a human
- doing what you say
- responding quickly
- saying when you don't know something
- dozens more…

But here some things to think about from a human behavior/brain science lens:

🙉 Hearing only engages your verbal system. Requires high cognitive load (brain turns conceptual words into images) and has 10-20% memory retention.

🙈 Seeing engages verbal, visual, and motor systems. Requires low cognitive load (visual images access 1/3 of our total brain power) and has 65-80% memory retention.

🙊 Doing engages full sensory and procedural systems. This requires the lowest cognitive load and results in 90%+ memory retention.


Think about this with your demo.

You can just talk through your product = hearing only = 10% retention = less memorable than most folks.

You can show your demo = seeing and hearing = 65% retention = as memorable than most folks.

You can have them build their own demo = full sensory engagement = 90% retention = more memorable than most folks.

More memorable = more talked about when you aren't in the room = win the deal.

Tools like Consensus help you build these interactive demos that just work better than the harbor tour you do now, where you drone on for 30 mins.

Align to your buyer by creating trust that only being memorable unlocks.

## Post 3
- **Date:** December 2025
- **Engagement:** 190 likes · 43 comments · 2 reposts
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mkosoglow_outbound-is-hard-agree-but-it-can-be-easy-activity-7386412205967196160-VeLj)
- **Why it matters:** Highest engagement post — draws a critical distinction between cold outreach and true outbound, and explains why warming prospects first changes everything

Outbound is hard. Agree. But it can be easy.

I know a company who had their best outbound qtr in a year. How?

They did outbound the right way. The patient way.

I was talking to the folks at AK Operations a few months back.

They reinforced what my mentor Marylou Tyler told me years ago.



Most people conflate (mix together) outbound and cold outreach.

VERY DIFFERENT.

Most companies say they are doing outbound, they aren't.

They are doing cold outreach, which sucks.

When someone says, "Outbound is dead," they usually don't mean that.

What they mean is "Cold outreach is dead."



Cold outreach = emailing strangers who aren't familiar with you.

Outbound = contacting warmed up people who show signs of interest.



When I started sequencing folks, cold outreach still worked.

I'd send 100 emails to TOTAL STRANGERS.

They had no clue who I was, or what I did.

Hit on a problem they had → 10-15 replies, positive replies, meetings.

That was more than 10 years ago.

Send 100 cold outbound emails today → maybe book 1 meetings.

But, teams like AKOPs are getting reply rates of 6%+.

How?



They understand the diff between cold outreach and outbound.

They refuse to do (or let their clients talk them into) cold outreach.

It sucks. It doesn't work.

Instead, they work hard to get their clients to do outbound the right way.

They spend a quarter warming up prospects they want to outbound to.

Great content - check.
No CTAs - check.
Patience - check, in truckloads.
Value every touch - check.
Opinions and ideas - check.
Special content pieces - check.
Extreme personalization - check.
Warming up the prospect for outbound so it's not cold outreach - BOOM!


Want to really set up an amazing n8n or Clay workflow to go outbound?

Then, you need to nurture (old school term that has been done so poorly it's got a bad reputation) cold prospects so that you can outbound to them.

Your first sequence to a prospect in tools like lemlist is a meat thermometer.

Stick it in their world and let it sit for a second.

It'll tell you if they are ready or not.

If not, let that burger (prospect) sit on the grill a bit longer.

Don't take it off until it's deliciously medium rare.

Then, you start outbounding.

Asking for a meeting with your first email is like eating a raw burger.

Gross. For you AND your prospect.

That first sequence needs to create signals.

Get enough signals and you know the prospect is out of the "cold outreach" phase and ready to be "outbounded" to.

Thick, juicy, yummy burgers (or dried meats)…that's what outbound is like eating.

Once you start, you'll never stop. You'll get fat and happy!

## Post 4
- **Date:** October 2025
- **Engagement:** 41 likes · 7 comments
- **Post URL:** [View on LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/mkosoglow_think-saas-reps-give-1000000-demos-per-activity-7384570083316039681-D_IL)
- **Why it matters:** Reframes demos as a two-way learning exercise — most reps only focus on teaching, missing half the value of every demo conversation

Think SaaS reps give 1,000,000 demos per day? Seems reasonable.

How many do you think the rep believe went well? 😅

If you said 1,000,000, you'd probably not be far off.

I've heard almost every rep, after almost every demo, answer the question, "How'd it go?" with "Good."

But, most reps are looking at one end of the equation to answer that.

Every demo has 2 parts:

1. what you taught
2. what you learned

Only the very best reps take into account what they learned in a demo when answering, "How'd it go?"

What happens when you ignore:
- half the discovery?
- half the negotiations?
- half the onboarding reqs?
- half the buying committee?

You lose and fail.

That's what's happening with your demo if you ignore the learning half.

What should you learn from a demo?

Here's are 5 (and tools like Consensus make this a ton easier and automatic):

1. What does each member of the buying group care about → imagine seeing which topics, workflows, screens in your demo different personas and execs were most interested in.

2. What confused people who saw your demo → would they rewatch parts, re-do click throughs? Your demo keeps going but what if you knew where to rework, spend more time?

3. How to pinpoint your discovery before the demo → the best parts of your product may be different than you think they are. Knowing what excites people the most can inform where you need to spend more time in discovery.

4. Where your website needs more emphasis → once you know where people in demos get pumped and want to spend more time, your marketing team can beef up those parts of the site which generates more leads. Same for where they are confused.

5. Who your buying committee shares demos with → it's hard to get everyone that influences a decision what they need to pick you, but if you can see the wider buying committee…the hidden folks…you can make sure to include them.



When you demo, there is a potential gap in understanding that is created.

As you continue to demo, the gap may increase.

Most reps "feel" this, if they have good instincts.

So, they ask something like, "Does that make sense?"

We've all heard that's a bad questions.

But it's only a bad question if it's your ONLY question.

If you understand that question is your sales DNA telling you there's an understanding gap, then you can ask a question that makes the buyer demonstrate their understanding of what they just saw.

Example for at Docebo - "How much more content do you think your team could create with our Content Creator AI?"

If their answer shows understanding, you can keep going.

If it doesn't, you have to start over.

You need to LEARN in a demo, as much as TEACH.

Never stop learning.

Just because you look at data to figure out what to do doesn't mean you make data-driven decisions. Data helps make wrong decisions more than it helps make right ones, and AI makes it worse.

GIGO = Garbage In, Garbage Out.

Most people know this, but that don't understand it.

They think if you put in bad data, you get bad data out.

While true, what is more often the case is putting in inconsistent data leads to bad data out.

Perfect example is your sales process.

You have 5, 6, 7 stages, but nobody uses them the same way.
- Rep A puts deals in stage 2 when she feels they are qualified.
- Rep B puts deals in stage 2 after the demo and they book a next mtg.
- Rep C doesn't use stage 2 (he's a Stage 1 → Won kinda guy).

Imagine this across 20, 50, 100 reps.

Your CEO asks you to figure out why win rates are down.

You grab stage-to-stage conversion data.

Run some numbers, get a spreadsheet going, ask ChatGPT.

But no tool, no genius, and no AI can give you valuable insights.

Why?

Because the data isn't normalized...reps don't use stages for the same reason.

Rep A, B, and C aren't giving you bad data bc they are consistent in how they use stages.

They are giving you inconsistent data that doesn't tell you how good you are at moving deals into stage 2 from a macro/team-wide perspective. 

But, you grab the data and start fixing something that isn't really a problem bc it looks like a problem in your data.

Then, you unleash AI on your CRM data. Ouch. 

AI can do lots of stuff, but it can't figure out that your reps don't use the stages differently.

So, it gives you the "answers" but they don't help.
